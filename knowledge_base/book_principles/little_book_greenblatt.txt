THE LITTLE BOOK THAT BEATS THE MARKET — Joel Greenblatt
Key Principles for Analyzing Companies

=== THE MAGIC FORMULA ===

Greenblatt's Magic Formula ranks stocks on two factors, combining quality with value:

1. EARNINGS YIELD = EBIT / Enterprise Value
   - Measures how cheap the business is
   - Uses EBIT (not net income) to compare companies regardless of capital structure
   - Uses Enterprise Value (market cap + debt - cash) to account for leverage
   - Higher is better: you're getting more earnings per dollar invested
   - In annual reports: Find EBIT on the income statement, compute EV from balance sheet + market price

2. RETURN ON CAPITAL = EBIT / (Net Fixed Assets + Net Working Capital)
   - Measures how good the business is at deploying capital
   - Higher is better: the business generates more profit per dollar of capital employed
   - In annual reports: Find net fixed assets and net working capital on the balance sheet
   - Consistently high ROC suggests durable competitive advantages

The Magic Formula ranks stocks by both metrics and buys those that rank highest on both (cheap AND good).

=== WHY THE MAGIC FORMULA WORKS ===

"Buying good businesses at bargain prices is the secret to making lots of money."

The formula works because:
- HIGH EARNINGS YIELD means you're buying cheap relative to the business's earning power
- HIGH RETURN ON CAPITAL means you're buying a genuinely good business
- Together, they identify quality businesses temporarily available at discount prices

=== GREENBLATT ON WHAT MAKES A GOOD BUSINESS ===

"A good business is one that earns a high return on its capital."

In annual reports, look for:
- ROIC consistently above 20% over many years
- This indicates competitive advantages (moats) — a business without advantages can't sustain high returns because competition would erode them
- "If a business earns 25% on capital, and you can reinvest in the business at those rates, you have a compounding machine."

Why return on capital matters more than growth:
- A business growing at 15% but earning 5% on capital is destroying value
- A business growing at 5% but earning 25% on capital is creating enormous value
- "Growth is great, but only if the business can grow while maintaining high returns on capital."

=== GREENBLATT ON ENTERPRISE VALUE ===

"Looking at a company's earnings yield based on enterprise value gives a clearer picture than P/E ratio."

Why EV-based metrics are superior (found in annual reports):
- P/E ignores debt: A company with $10B market cap and $20B debt is NOT cheaper than one with $10B market cap and no debt
- Enterprise Value = Market Cap + Debt - Cash
- EBIT/EV compares companies on a capital-structure-neutral basis
- This prevents the trap of buying highly leveraged companies that LOOK cheap on P/E

=== GREENBLATT ON SPECIAL SITUATIONS ===

Greenblatt also wrote "You Can Be a Stock Market Genius" focusing on special situations visible in annual reports:

1. SPIN-OFFS: When a company spins off a division, the spin-off is often mispriced.
   - Institutional investors often dump the spin-off because it's too small or doesn't fit their mandate
   - Read the prospectus/annual report of the new entity carefully
   - "Spin-offs, on average, outperform the market by about 10% per year in their first three years."

2. RECAPITALIZATIONS: When companies restructure their balance sheet.
   - Look for companies distributing excess cash while maintaining strong operations

3. MERGER SECURITIES: The securities received in mergers are often sold indiscriminately.

4. RIGHTS OFFERINGS: Usually cause temporary price drops as shareholders sell.

=== APPLYING THE MAGIC FORMULA TO ANNUAL REPORTS ===

When reading an annual report through Greenblatt's lens, focus on:

1. QUALITY (Return on Capital):
   - Calculate EBIT / (Net Fixed Assets + Net Working Capital)
   - Is this ratio consistently high (>20%)?
   - Has it been maintained over 5-10 years?
   - If ROC is declining, the moat may be eroding

2. VALUE (Earnings Yield):
   - Calculate EBIT / Enterprise Value
   - Is the yield attractive compared to bonds and the market average?
   - Use normalized earnings (average over a cycle) for cyclical businesses

3. SUSTAINABILITY:
   - Why does this business earn high returns?
   - What protects these returns from competition?
   - Read the Risk Factors and competitive discussion in the annual report

4. CAPITAL ALLOCATION:
   - What does management do with the excess cash from high returns?
   - Reinvest at similar high rates? (Best outcome)
   - Return to shareholders? (Good outcome)
   - Waste on bad acquisitions? (Bad outcome)

=== GREENBLATT'S KEY INSIGHT ===

"The magic formula works because it forces you to buy above-average companies at below-average prices. Most investors either buy good companies at any price (growth investors) or cheap companies of any quality (deep value investors). The combination is more powerful than either alone."
