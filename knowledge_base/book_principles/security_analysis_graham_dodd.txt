SECURITY ANALYSIS — Benjamin Graham & David Dodd
Key Principles for Analyzing Annual Reports

=== THE FOUNDATION OF FUNDAMENTAL ANALYSIS ===

Security Analysis is the foundational text for analyzing financial statements. Graham and Dodd taught that the annual report is the primary tool for determining intrinsic value.

"The analyst's job is to determine what a security is worth, not what it will sell for."

=== EARNINGS ANALYSIS ===

Graham and Dodd distinguished between different types of earnings:

1. REPORTED EARNINGS: What the income statement shows. Often unreliable because of accounting choices.

2. REAL EARNINGS: The actual economic earning power after adjustments:
   - Remove non-recurring gains and losses
   - Normalize for the business cycle (use 7-10 year averages)
   - Adjust for accounting method changes
   - Deduct stock-based compensation at fair value
   - Include maintenance capex as a real expense

3. EARNING POWER: The sustainable, normalized earnings the business can produce in average conditions.
   - "Earning power is a concept of the past applied to the future."
   - Use average earnings, not peak or trough earnings
   - For cyclical businesses, earning power should be based on mid-cycle results

=== BALANCE SHEET ANALYSIS ===

"The balance sheet deserves more attention than it usually gets."

Key Graham & Dodd balance sheet techniques for annual reports:

1. NET CURRENT ASSET VALUE (NCAV):
   - NCAV = Current Assets - ALL Liabilities (not just current)
   - If stock trades below NCAV, you're getting the business for less than its liquidation value
   - This provides extreme margin of safety

2. TANGIBLE BOOK VALUE:
   - Book Value - Intangible Assets (goodwill, patents, etc.)
   - Intangibles should be viewed skeptically: "Goodwill has value only if the business generates excess returns"
   - Companies trading below tangible book value deserve investigation

3. WORKING CAPITAL TRENDS:
   - Is working capital growing in line with revenue?
   - Rapid growth in receivables may signal aggressive revenue recognition
   - Rapid growth in inventory may signal demand problems
   - Declining working capital may signal cash flow issues

4. HIDDEN ASSETS AND LIABILITIES:
   - Real estate carried at historical cost (may be worth far more)
   - LIFO inventory reserves (understates true inventory value)
   - Pension obligations (may be underfunded)
   - Operating lease obligations (effectively debt)
   - Environmental liabilities
   - Lawsuit contingencies

=== INCOME STATEMENT ANALYSIS ===

"The income statement is more subject to manipulation than the balance sheet."

What to examine in annual reports:

1. REVENUE QUALITY:
   - Is revenue growing organically or through acquisitions?
   - Revenue recognition: When does the company book revenue? Upfront or over time?
   - Are there unusual revenue items that inflate the top line?
   - Recurring vs. non-recurring revenue

2. EXPENSE ANALYSIS:
   - Depreciation: Is the depreciation policy realistic? Compare to capex.
   - If depreciation << capex, the company may be under-depreciating
   - SG&A as a percentage of revenue: Is it growing or shrinking?
   - Are there "restructuring charges" every year? Then they're not really restructuring charges.

3. MARGIN ANALYSIS:
   - Gross margin: Measures pricing power and production efficiency
   - Operating margin: Includes the cost of running the business
   - Net margin: After all costs, taxes, and interest
   - Trend matters more than absolute level

=== CASH FLOW ANALYSIS ===

Graham and Dodd emphasized cash over earnings:

"In the long run, a company's value is determined by its cash flows, not its reported earnings."

1. OPERATING CASH FLOW vs NET INCOME:
   - If net income consistently exceeds operating cash flow, earnings quality is suspect
   - Cash flow is harder to manipulate than earnings
   - "Cash flow is the reality check on reported earnings."

2. FREE CASH FLOW:
   - FCF = Operating Cash Flow - Capital Expenditures
   - A company that can't generate free cash flow is consuming capital, not creating it
   - Free cash flow funds dividends, buybacks, debt repayment, and acquisitions

3. CAPITAL EXPENDITURE ANALYSIS:
   - Maintenance capex vs. growth capex
   - If capex consistently exceeds depreciation, the company may be under-depreciating
   - Capital-intensive businesses require more reinvestment, leaving less for shareholders

=== COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS ===

"No company should be analyzed in isolation."

Graham and Dodd advocated comparing:
- The company's metrics to industry peers
- The company's current metrics to its own history
- The company's management claims to actual results

In annual reports, compare:
- Margins to competitors (disclosed in industry analysis)
- Growth rates to industry averages
- Return on capital to peers
- Debt levels to peers
- This year's promises to last year's delivery

=== GRAHAM & DODD'S WARNINGS ===

Red flags when reading annual reports:

- EARNINGS MANAGEMENT: Companies that consistently report earnings just above analyst estimates
- CHANNEL STUFFING: Pushing product to distributors to inflate revenue (shows up as inventory buildup at customers or accelerating receivables)
- COOKIE JAR RESERVES: Taking large write-offs in bad years, then releasing reserves in good years to smooth earnings
- PRO FORMA EARNINGS: Non-GAAP measures that exclude real, recurring expenses
- ACQUISITION ACCOUNTING: Using acquisition reserves to inflate future earnings
- PENSION MANIPULATION: Using unrealistic return assumptions to reduce pension expense
- "Accounting quality is as important as business quality."
