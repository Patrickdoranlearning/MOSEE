POOR CHARLIE'S ALMANACK — Charlie Munger
The Wit and Wisdom of Charles T. Munger

=== THE LATTICEWORK OF MENTAL MODELS ===

Munger's central thesis: You need multiple mental models from multiple disciplines to make good decisions. Relying on one framework leads to the "man with a hammer" syndrome — "To a man with only a hammer, every problem looks like a nail."

Essential mental models for analyzing annual reports:

1. ENGINEERING — REDUNDANCY & BACKUP SYSTEMS:
   - Does the company have backup systems? Multiple revenue streams? Diversified customers?
   - A business dependent on a single product, customer, or geography has no redundancy
   - "Engineering systems have multiple backups. The best businesses do too."

2. MATHEMATICS — COMPOUND INTEREST:
   - "Understanding both the power of compound interest and the difficulty of getting it is the heart and soul of understanding a lot of things."
   - Look for businesses that can reinvest earnings at high rates of return
   - A company earning 20% ROE that retains all earnings doubles in value every 3.6 years
   - The eighth wonder of the world, but only if the business can maintain those returns

3. MATHEMATICS — PROBABILITY & DECISION TREES:
   - Don't think about the most likely outcome. Think about ALL possible outcomes weighted by probability.
   - Expected value = Sum of (probability × outcome) for all scenarios
   - When reading annual reports, map out bull case, base case, and bear case scenarios

4. PSYCHOLOGY — INCENTIVE STRUCTURES:
   - "Never, ever, think about something else when you should be thinking about the power of incentives."
   - Management compensation structure determines behavior
   - If the CEO gets bonuses for revenue growth, expect acquisitions regardless of value
   - If stock options vest regardless of performance, expect dilution

5. BIOLOGY — EVOLUTION & ADAPTATION:
   - Businesses must adapt or die, just like organisms
   - Look for companies that are evolving: investing in R&D, entering new markets, adapting to technology
   - Companies clinging to legacy business models in changing industries are the corporate equivalent of dinosaurs

6. PHYSICS — CRITICAL MASS:
   - Some businesses need to reach critical mass before they become self-sustaining
   - Network effects: Each new user makes the network more valuable for all users
   - Platform businesses often exhibit winner-take-most dynamics

7. ECONOMICS — COMPETITIVE DESTRUCTION:
   - "In capitalism, it's a lot like a chess game. You have to be in a position where you can't be easily taken."
   - Study the five forces: rivalry, new entrants, substitutes, supplier power, buyer power
   - Annual reports reveal which forces are intensifying and which are weakening

=== THE PSYCHOLOGY OF HUMAN MISJUDGMENT — FULL FRAMEWORK ===

Munger identified 25 cognitive biases. The most relevant for analyzing annual reports:

1. REWARD AND PUNISHMENT SUPER-RESPONSE:
   - People respond to incentives, often unconsciously
   - In annual reports: How is management paid? Short-term bonuses create short-term thinking
   - "Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome."

2. LIKING/LOVING TENDENCY:
   - We tend to ignore faults in things/people we like
   - Investors fall in love with stocks and ignore deteriorating fundamentals
   - When reading annual reports of companies you own, actively look for negatives

3. DISLIKING/HATING TENDENCY:
   - We tend to ignore virtues in things/people we dislike
   - Don't dismiss a company because of one bad quarter or an unlikable CEO
   - Read the numbers, not the narrative

4. DOUBT-AVOIDANCE TENDENCY:
   - People rush to remove doubt by making quick decisions
   - Don't rush to a conclusion after reading one annual report
   - Read multiple years. Read competitors. Take your time.

5. INCONSISTENCY-AVOIDANCE TENDENCY:
   - Once we form a view, we resist changing it
   - If you bought a stock at $100 and it's now $50, re-read the annual report as if you didn't own it
   - "The human mind is a lot like the human egg. When one idea gets in, it shuts the gate."

6. OVER-OPTIMISM TENDENCY:
   - Management's MD&A section is almost always overly optimistic
   - Discount management projections by 20-30%
   - Look for concrete data, not aspirational statements

7. SOCIAL-PROOF TENDENCY:
   - "Monkey see, monkey do." Companies imitate competitors.
   - When every company in an industry is doing the same thing (expanding, buying back stock, acquiring), be skeptical
   - The best companies think independently

8. AUTHORITY-MISINFLUENCE TENDENCY:
   - Don't blindly trust management because they're executives
   - Verify their claims against the numbers
   - "Trust, but verify."

9. TWADDLE TENDENCY:
   - People talk and write more than they should
   - Annual reports filled with buzzwords and jargon ("synergies," "best-in-class," "transformational") are often covering up a lack of substance
   - "If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough."

10. DEPRIVAL SUPER-REACTION:
    - People react more strongly to losing something than to gaining it
    - Companies will destroy value to protect market share they're losing
    - Watch for irrational price wars or overpaying to keep customers

=== MUNGER'S INVESTMENT PRINCIPLES ===

"All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there."

INVERSION APPLIED TO ANNUAL REPORTS:
Instead of asking "Is this a good investment?", ask:
- What would make this a terrible investment?
- How could I lose all my money here?
- What am I missing that the market might know?
- What assumptions am I making that could be wrong?

THE FOUR FILTERS (Munger's version):
1. Do I understand this business? (If the annual report confuses you, pass)
2. Does it have a sustainable competitive advantage? (Moats)
3. Is management trustworthy and talented? (Integrity + competence)
4. Is the price reasonable? (Margin of safety)

"We have to deal in things we're capable of understanding, and then once we're in that territory, we need to buy the strongest stuff we can find."

=== MUNGER ON WORLDLY WISDOM ===

"You've got to have models in your head. And you've got to array your experience — both vicarious and direct — on this latticework of models."

Key principles for annual report analysis:

CIRCLE OF COMPETENCE:
- Only analyze businesses you truly understand
- "Knowing what you don't know is more useful than being brilliant."
- If you can't explain how the company makes money after reading the annual report, move on

THE LOLLAPALOOZA EFFECT:
- When multiple mental models point in the same direction, the effect is multiplicative, not additive
- A company with great management + strong moat + growing market + low valuation = lollapalooza
- Conversely, bad management + deteriorating moat + shrinking market + high valuation = disaster

PATIENCE:
- "The big money is not in the buying and the selling, but in the waiting."
- Don't feel compelled to act after reading every annual report
- The best opportunities require patience: waiting for quality at a fair price

=== MUNGER ON CORPORATE GOVERNANCE ===

What to look for in annual reports regarding governance:

1. BOARD INDEPENDENCE: Are directors truly independent or cronies of the CEO?
2. COMPENSATION FAIRNESS: Is the CEO paid like an owner or like a king?
3. CAPITAL ALLOCATION: Is the board rubber-stamping management decisions?
4. SHAREHOLDER ALIGNMENT: Do directors own meaningful amounts of stock?
5. TRANSPARENCY: Does the company voluntarily disclose more than required?

"A lot of people think that if you just get governance right, everything follows. That's not true. You need to get the culture right."

=== MUNGER'S CHECKLIST FOR AVOIDING MISTAKES ===

Before investing, check each item:
- Have I accounted for all relevant facts?
- Have I thought about this from the other side (inversion)?
- Am I being influenced by social proof?
- Am I anchored to a previous price or opinion?
- Am I overweighting recent events?
- Have I considered the full range of outcomes, not just the most likely?
- Is my reasoning sound, or am I rationalizing a decision I've already made?
- Would I be comfortable if this investment were made public?
