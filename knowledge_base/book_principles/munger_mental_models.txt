CHARLIE MUNGER'S MENTAL MODELS AND INVESTMENT WISDOM
From Poor Charlie's Almanack, speeches, and Berkshire meetings

=== THE PSYCHOLOGY OF HUMAN MISJUDGMENT ===

Munger identified cognitive biases that cause investors and managers to make poor decisions. When reading annual reports, watch for these biases in management behavior:

1. INCENTIVE-CAUSED BIAS: "Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome."
   - How is management compensated? If bonuses are tied to revenue growth, expect acquisitions. If tied to EPS, expect buybacks and accounting tricks.
   - Read the proxy statement / compensation section carefully.
   - Are incentives aligned with long-term shareholder value?

2. COMMITMENT AND CONSISTENCY BIAS: Once management commits to a strategy publicly, they'll stick with it even when evidence says it's failing.
   - Compare strategic direction described in annual reports over multiple years
   - Are they doubling down on failed strategies?
   - Do they pivot when facts change, or ride losing bets?

3. SOCIAL PROOF: Management imitating competitors rather than thinking independently.
   - "Whenever you hear the phrase 'the industry standard practice,' be skeptical."
   - Are acquisitions driven by competitive pressure rather than value creation?

4. DEPRIVAL SUPER-REACTION: Companies often make terrible decisions to avoid losing something.
   - Overpaying to retain market share
   - Destroying value to prevent competitors from gaining ground

5. LOLLAPALOOZA EFFECTS: Multiple biases combining to create extreme outcomes.
   - The most dangerous situations are when several biases reinforce each other

=== INVERSION ===

"Invert, always invert." Munger's most powerful mental model.

Instead of asking "What makes this a good investment?", ask:
- What could destroy this business?
- What could go wrong?
- Why might this investment fail?
- What would make me sell?

When reading annual reports, deliberately look for:
- The risks that could kill the business
- The weaknesses management is not discussing
- The ways the competitive position could erode
- The scenarios where the debt becomes unserviceable

=== MUNGER ON MOATS ===

"How do you compete against a true fanatic? You can only try to build the best moat you can around your own business."

Types of moats Munger valued:
1. BRAND: "Coca-Cola's moat is in the mind of the consumer."
2. SCALE: "Being the low-cost producer in a commodity business is like having a license to steal."
3. SWITCHING COSTS: "If it costs the customer more to switch than to stay, you have a moat."
4. NETWORK EFFECTS: "A telephone system becomes more valuable as more people join it."
5. REGULATORY: "Some of the best businesses have regulatory protection."

=== MUNGER ON MANAGEMENT ===

"The best thing a human being can do is to help another human being know more."

What Munger looked for in management:
- RATIONALITY above all: "The most important quality in a manager is rationality, not brilliance."
- CANDOR: Do they admit mistakes? "I like people who admit their mistakes. It shows they know more than when they started."
- CAPITAL ALLOCATION SKILL: "If a management can't allocate capital well, even the best business will suffer."
- SKIN IN THE GAME: Managers should be significant shareholders.
- SIMPLICITY: Good managers keep things simple. Complexity is often a warning sign.

=== MUNGER ON ACCOUNTING ===

"People who know nothing about accounting have no business investing."

Red flags Munger watched for:
- EBITDA obsession: "Every time you hear EBITDA, substitute 'bullshit earnings.'"
- Pro-forma adjustments that always make earnings look better
- Pension fund assumptions that are unrealistically optimistic
- Goodwill that never gets written down despite poor acquisition performance
- Companies where reported earnings consistently exceed cash flow
- "Three of the worst words in investing are 'special charges expected.'"

=== MUNGER'S INVESTMENT CHECKLIST ===

Before investing, Munger required:
1. A business he understood ("We have three baskets: in, out, and too hard")
2. Intrinsic competitive advantages with durability
3. Able and trustworthy management
4. Available at a price that makes sense (margin of safety)
5. A business that can reinvest at high rates of return

=== MUNGER ON WHAT MAKES A GREAT BUSINESS ===

"A great business at a fair price is superior to a fair business at a great price."

Characteristics of a great business from annual reports:
- High returns on capital WITHOUT leverage
- Ability to raise prices without losing customers
- Minimal capital requirements for growth
- Earnings that are highly predictable
- Protected from competition by durable advantages
- Management that is rational about capital allocation

=== MULTIDISCIPLINARY THINKING ===

Munger advocated using multiple mental models from different disciplines:
- Economics: Supply and demand, competitive destruction, scale advantages
- Psychology: Behavioral biases, incentives, social proof
- Mathematics: Compound interest, probability, statistics
- Engineering: Redundancy, backup systems, margin of safety
- Biology: Evolution, adaptation, ecosystem thinking

When analyzing an annual report, don't just think like an accountant. Think about:
- The competitive ecosystem (biology)
- The management incentives (psychology)
- The probability of different outcomes (mathematics)
- The economic moat (economics)
