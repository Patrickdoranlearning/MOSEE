ONE UP ON WALL STREET — Peter Lynch
Key Principles for Analyzing Companies

=== LYNCH'S SIX CATEGORIES OF STOCKS ===

Lynch classified every stock into one of six categories. When reading annual reports, first determine which category the company belongs to, because each category has different evaluation criteria:

1. SLOW GROWERS (Sluggards): Large, mature companies growing slightly faster than GDP (2-4%).
   - Expected in mature industries
   - Valued for dividends, not growth
   - In annual reports: Look for steady dividends, stable but unexciting revenue
   - Red flag: Management talking about "exciting growth opportunities" in a clearly mature business

2. STALWARTS: Large companies with moderate growth (10-12%). Think Coca-Cola, Procter & Gamble.
   - Good protection in recessions
   - In annual reports: Look for consistent earnings growth, expanding margins, global expansion
   - Buy when P/E is below growth rate; sell when P/E significantly exceeds growth rate

3. FAST GROWERS: Small, aggressive companies growing 20-25% per year.
   - The most rewarding category
   - In annual reports: Look for rapid revenue growth, improving margins, expanding into new markets
   - Red flag: Growth funded entirely by debt, or growth slowing while management denies it
   - "The key question: How long can the company keep growing, and how much has the market already paid for that growth?"

4. CYCLICALS: Companies whose sales and profits rise and fall in regular patterns (autos, airlines, steel, chemicals).
   - Timing is everything
   - In annual reports: Look at where they are in the cycle. Low earnings and low P/E = buy. High earnings and low P/E = SELL.
   - "With cyclicals, you buy them when the P/E ratio is high (earnings depressed) and sell when the P/E is low (earnings at peak)."

5. TURNAROUNDS: Companies recovering from disaster.
   - In annual reports: Look for debt reduction, cost cutting, asset sales, new management
   - "The best turnarounds are the ones with the least debt."
   - How much cash do they have? Can they survive long enough to recover?

6. ASSET PLAYS: Companies sitting on valuable assets the market hasn't noticed.
   - In annual reports: Look at the balance sheet for undervalued real estate, natural resources, brand value, patents
   - "The assets can be anything from cash and real estate to brand names and patents."

=== THE PEG RATIO ===

Lynch's signature metric:

PEG Ratio = P/E Ratio / Earnings Growth Rate

- PEG < 1.0: Stock is undervalued relative to its growth
- PEG = 1.0: Fairly valued
- PEG > 1.5: Getting expensive
- PEG > 2.0: Usually overvalued

In annual reports, estimate the sustainable earnings growth rate by:
- Looking at the historical EPS growth rate (5-10 years)
- Checking if growth is accelerating or decelerating
- Assessing whether growth is organic or acquisition-driven
- Comparing to management's own guidance

=== LYNCH'S 2-MINUTE DRILL ===

Lynch could quickly assess any company by answering these questions from the annual report:

1. What's the company's story? Can you explain it in a simple sentence?
2. Is revenue growing? How fast?
3. Is EPS growing? How fast?
4. What's the P/E relative to growth rate?
5. Is the balance sheet strong (cash > debt)?
6. Are insiders buying?
7. Is the company buying back shares?

=== WHAT LYNCH LOOKED FOR IN ANNUAL REPORTS ===

1. CASH POSITION: "Cash is King. If a company has a lot of cash per share relative to its stock price, that's very bullish."
   - Net cash = Cash - Total Debt
   - Net cash per share relative to stock price
   - Is cash growing or shrinking?

2. DEBT LEVELS: Lynch preferred companies with low debt.
   - Debt-to-equity ratio
   - Interest coverage
   - "In a recession, companies with a lot of debt get killed."

3. INVENTORY: Especially for manufacturers and retailers.
   - Is inventory growing faster than sales? (Bad sign — they can't sell what they make)
   - Is inventory declining relative to sales? (Good sign — products are selling well)

4. EARNINGS GROWTH CONSISTENCY: "One year of great earnings followed by a year of terrible earnings is not as valuable as steady, consistent growth."

5. PROFIT MARGINS: Are they expanding, stable, or contracting?
   - Expanding margins + growing revenue = the sweet spot
   - Contracting margins are an early warning sign

=== LYNCH'S RED FLAGS ===

- Hot stocks in hot industries: "If I could avoid a single stock, it would be the hottest stock in the hottest industry."
- Companies that diversify through acquisitions: "Diworsification"
- Companies dependent on a single customer or product
- "Whisper stocks" with exciting stories but no earnings
- Companies where the stock price has gotten way ahead of fundamentals
- Excessive hype in the annual report without substance
- Companies spending more time on investor relations than on their actual business

=== LYNCH ON THE IDEAL COMPANY ===

The perfect Lynch investment has these characteristics visible in annual reports:
1. The name is boring or sounds ridiculous
2. The business is boring or disagreeable
3. It's a spinoff
4. Institutions don't own it
5. There's something depressing about it
6. The industry is no-growth
7. It has a niche
8. People have to keep buying (razor blades, not one-time purchases)
9. It's a user of technology, not a maker
10. Insiders are buying
11. The company is buying back shares
12. It has a strong cash position

=== LYNCH ON EARNINGS ===

"In the end, the earnings will determine the stock price."

When assessing earnings from annual reports:
- Look at the 5-10 year earnings trend line
- Ask: Are earnings growing because of real business improvement or accounting tricks?
- Compare earnings growth to revenue growth — if earnings grow much faster than revenue, it may not be sustainable
- Check the tax rate — a one-time tax benefit can inflate earnings temporarily
- "The p/e ratio of any company that is fairly priced will equal its growth rate."
