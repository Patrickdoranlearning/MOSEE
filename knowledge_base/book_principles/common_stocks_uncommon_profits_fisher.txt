COMMON STOCKS AND UNCOMMON PROFITS — Philip A. Fisher
Key Principles for Analyzing Companies

=== FISHER'S 15 POINTS TO LOOK FOR IN A COMMON STOCK ===

When reading a company's annual report and researching a potential investment, Fisher evaluated these 15 dimensions. Each can be partially assessed from annual report filings:

1. GROWTH POTENTIAL: Does the company have products or services with sufficient market potential to make possible a sizable increase in sales for at least several years?
   - Look at revenue growth trajectory in the income statement
   - Read the MD&A for management's discussion of addressable market
   - Is the company gaining or losing market share?

2. MANAGEMENT'S COMMITMENT TO GROWTH: Does management have a determination to continue developing products or processes that will further increase total sales potential?
   - Look for R&D spending as a percentage of revenue
   - Are they investing in new products, or milking existing ones?
   - Read about capital expenditure plans

3. R&D EFFECTIVENESS: How effective are the company's research and development efforts in relation to its size?
   - Compare R&D spend to competitors
   - What products or innovations has R&D produced recently?
   - Is R&D spending growing or shrinking?

4. SALES ORGANIZATION: Does the company have an above-average sales organization?
   - Look at revenue per employee trends
   - Is the company gaining customers or losing them?
   - What does customer retention look like?

5. PROFIT MARGINS: Does the company have a worthwhile profit margin?
   - Track gross margin, operating margin, and net margin over time
   - Are margins expanding or contracting?
   - How do margins compare to industry peers?

6. IMPROVING MARGINS: What is the company doing to maintain or improve profit margins?
   - Look for discussion of cost management, pricing power, efficiency
   - Is the company investing to reduce costs?
   - Scale advantages showing up in margins?

7. LABOR RELATIONS: Does the company have outstanding labor and personnel relations?
   - Read about employee satisfaction, turnover, labor disputes
   - Does the company invest in training and development?
   - "A company in which there is a well-established feeling among its workers that it is a good place to work is a company in which there is likely to be an esprit de corps."

8. EXECUTIVE RELATIONS: Does the company have outstanding executive relations?
   - Is there depth of management, or is success dependent on one person?
   - Are executives promoted from within?
   - Is there a clear succession plan?

9. MANAGEMENT DEPTH: Does the company have depth to its management?
   - "The company that can grow only as far as its founder can personally direct operations will not grow very far."
   - Look for evidence of delegated authority and talented lieutenants

10. COST ANALYSIS: How good are the company's cost analysis and accounting controls?
    - Does the company break out segment reporting clearly?
    - Can you understand where profits come from?
    - Are cost structures transparent?

11. COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGES: Are there other aspects of the business that give the investor clues as to how outstanding the company may be in relation to its competition?
    - Patents, proprietary technology, regulatory advantages
    - Customer switching costs
    - Network effects, platform advantages

12. LONG-RANGE OUTLOOK ON PROFITS: Does the company have a short-range or long-range outlook in regard to profits?
    - Is management sacrificing long-term value for short-term earnings?
    - Are they investing heavily now for future growth?
    - Do they discuss 3-5 year plans, or only quarter-to-quarter?

13. EQUITY DILUTION: Will growth require equity financing that will dilute existing shareholders?
    - Look at shares outstanding over time — is it growing?
    - Stock-based compensation levels
    - Are buybacks genuine return of capital or just offsetting dilution from options?

14. MANAGEMENT COMMUNICATION: Does management talk freely about its affairs when things are going well but clam up when troubles and disappointments occur?
    - "The management that candidly discusses its problems is the one that usually solves them."
    - Compare tone of this year's letter with last year's
    - Do they address disappointments honestly?

15. MANAGEMENT INTEGRITY: Does the company have a management of unquestionable integrity?
    - Related party transactions
    - Insider trading patterns
    - Does management enrich itself at shareholder expense?

=== THE SCUTTLEBUTT METHOD ===

Fisher advocated extensive qualitative research beyond annual reports:
- Talk to competitors about the company
- Talk to customers about the company
- Talk to suppliers about the company
- Talk to former employees

While we can't do this from annual reports alone, AI can look for:
- Customer concentration (revenue dependency on few customers)
- Supplier mentions and relationships
- Competitor mentions and positioning
- Industry dynamics discussed in Risk Factors

=== FISHER ON WHEN TO SELL ===

Fisher believed in holding great companies for very long periods. Sell only if:
1. You made a mistake in your original assessment
2. The company no longer qualifies on the 15 points
3. A significantly better opportunity exists

"If the job has been correctly done when a common stock is purchased, the time to sell it is — almost never."

=== FISHER ON GROWTH ===

"Sales growth is the engine that drives everything. A company cannot sustain growing profits without growing sales."

In annual reports, Fisher prioritized:
- Organic revenue growth (not acquisition-driven)
- Revenue CAGR over 5-10 years
- Whether growth is accelerating or decelerating
- The quality of growth (profitable growth vs unprofitable growth)
